Hampton · Feature

Fort Monroe and the birth of freedom: how contraband camps changed American history

Three enslaved men rowed to Union-held Fort Monroe in May 1861 — and the general who refused to return them created the legal crack that emancipation poured through.

Mara Whitfield · July 12 · 2 min read

Why it matters:

  • The "contraband of war" ruling at Fort Monroe became the first federal act to treat escaping enslaved people as free in practice.
  • It predates the Emancipation Proclamation by 20 months — and it happened here.

Driving the news:

  • The National Park Service this month expanded the Fort Monroe National Monument interpretive plan.
  • New archival records name Frank Baker, Shepard Mallory, and James Townsend — the three men whose crossing started it.

By the numbers:

  • 10,000+ people reached the "Freedom's Fortress" contraband camps by 1863.
  • 3 men in one rowboat began it on the night of May 23, 1861.

What's next:

  • A permanent exhibition opens at the Casemate Museum next spring; descendant families are contributing oral histories.
